## I. PRIVATE EQUITY & VENTURE CAPITAL

### A. Performance Characteristics

#### Historical Returns (2014-2025)

**Industry Claims vs. Reality:**

Private equity advocates report that PE delivered median annualized returns of 13.5-15.2% over 10-year periods ending in 2023-2024, outperforming every other asset class in public pension portfolios consistently since 2012.

However, **critical skepticism required**:

Independent research shows that between 2022 and Q3 2025, an index of US private equity funds saw annualized returns of only 5.8%, compared to 11.6% for the S&P 500. The top 50 pension funds in the country returned a 7.4% average over the decade ending June 30, 2024, while a plain vanilla 60/40 portfolio returned 8.1% over the same period.

**Performance by Time Period:**

| Period | Median PE Return | S&P 500 Return | Outperformance | Data Quality |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|----------------|--------------|
| 2014-2024 (10-yr) | 13.5-15.2%* | ~12% | +1.5 to +3.2% | Industry data (IRR) |
| 2022-Q3 2025 | 5.8% | 11.6% | -5.8% | MSCI Index |
| Since 2006 | ~Equal | ~Equal | 0% | PME basis |

*Industry figures use IRR methodology, which overstates returns vs. time-weighted returns

**Crisis Performance:**
- **2000-2002 Dot-com:** PE funds vintage 1999-2001 severely impaired; many <1x return
- **2008-2009 GFC:** PE outperformed public equities due to illiquidity "benefit" (no mark-to-market)
- **2020 COVID:** PE funds showed delayed markdowns; eventual returns strong for growth/tech-focused funds
- **2022 Inflation Shock:** CalPERS private equity returned 14.3% vs. 16.8% for public equity in FY2024-2025

**Recent Performance (FY2025):**
- CalPERS: Private equity best-performing asset class in calendar 2024; private debt 12.8%
- NYC pensions: Infrastructure 11.9%, alternative credit 9.5%
- Industry observation: "Private credit underperformed their public market equivalents (high yield and bank loans)" in 2024-2025

#### Volatility & Risk-Adjusted Returns

**Reported Volatility:** 18-22% (but artificially smoothed due to appraisal-based valuations)  
**True Economic Volatility:** Estimated 25-30% (comparable to or higher than public equity)  
**Sharpe Ratio:** 0.6-0.8 (industry claims) vs. 0.4-0.6 (independent estimates)

**Key Issue:** Since PE funds use appraisal-based valuations rather than market prices, volatility appears artificially low, and performance measurement is fundamentally flawed until exit.

### B. Diversification Benefits

**Correlation to Traditional Assets:**
- **Correlation to Public Equity:** 0.7-0.85 (high; PE returns largely driven by same economic factors)
- **Correlation to Bonds:** 0.1-0.3 (low)
- **Correlation to 60/40:** 0.5-0.7

**Stability Across Regimes:**
- **Low rate environment (2010-2021):** Strong PE performance, high correlation to tech/growth stocks
- **Rising rate environment (2022-2025):** PE struggled; correlation to public equity remained high
- **Inflation shocks:** Limited protection; PE portfolio companies face margin pressure

**Diversification Reality Check:**

PE does NOT provide meaningful diversification from public equity for the following reasons:
1. **Same underlying economics:** PE firms buy companies that would otherwise be public
2. **Leverage amplifies equity risk:** 4-7x leverage magnifies economic sensitivity
3. **Exit dependency:** Returns require buoyant public markets (IPOs or strategic sales)
4. **Reported correlation misleading:** Appraisal lag creates artificial decorrelation

**Does it protect during equity drawdowns?**

No. During market stress, PE suffers from both valuation pressure and exit market closure, though this may not show up in reported valuations for 6-12 months due to appraisal lag.

**Inflation protection?**

Weak. PE portfolio companies must manage input cost inflation and may lack pricing power. Leverage magnifies inflation's impact on cash flows.

### C. Return Drivers & Risk Factors

**Economic/Market Factors Driving Returns:**

1. **Beta (Market exposure):** 60-70% of returns
   - PE returns largely track public equity markets with leverage
   
2. **Alpha (Manager skill):** 20-30% of returns
   - Operational improvements, strategic repositioning
   - Extreme dispersion: top quartile meaningfully outperforms, bottom quartile destroys value
   
3. **Illiquidity Premium:** 5-10% of historical returns
   - Compensation for 10+ year lockup
   - Premium may have compressed as more capital entered asset class

4. **Leverage:** ~100-150 bps of return enhancement
   - Typical 50-60% LTV at acquisition
   - Magnifies both gains and losses

**Risk Factor Exposures:**

| Factor | Exposure Level | Impact |
|--------|---------------|--------|
| **GDP Growth** | Very High | PE heavily dependent on economic expansion |
| **Interest Rates** | Very High | High leverage makes PE sensitive to cost of capital |
| **Credit Spreads** | High | Exit valuations and refinancing depend on credit markets |
| **Equity Valuations** | Very High | Exit multiples track public market multiples |
| **Inflation** | Medium | Mixed; pricing power varies by company |
| **Liquidity** | Extreme | Returns depend on functioning exit markets |

**J-Curve Dynamics:**

Critical consideration for pension funds. Typical J-curve:
- **Years 1-3:** Negative cash flows (capital calls exceed distributions)
- **Years 4-7:** Cash flow neutral (capital calls = distributions)
- **Years 8-12:** Positive cash flows (distributions exceed remaining calls)

**Current Environment:** Exits declined significantly: 658 exits in 2022, 323 in 2023, 516 in 2024, and only 321 through December 2025 â€” well below the 2010-2020 average of 647 per year. This **extends the J-curve** and delays return of capital to LPs.

### D. Institutional Implementation Considerations

#### Minimum Allocation Size

**For adequate diversification:**
- Minimum 8-10% of total portfolio to justify governance burden
- Below 5%: insufficient to move the needle on returns
- Requires commitments to 15-20+ funds across multiple vintage years

**Capital deployment:**
- Pension fund our size ($2-5B AUM): $200-500M commitment program
- Deploy over 3-5 years: $40-100M per year
- Minimum fund commitment: $10-25M per fund (larger for mega-funds)

#### Liquidity Profile

**Lock-up periods:** 10-12 years typical (can extend to 15+ years)

**Capital call dynamics:**
- Commit $100M â†’ typically draw down over 3-5 years
- Peak exposure: Years 4-6 (most capital invested, limited distributions)
- "Denominator effect" risk: If public markets fall, PE allocation rises as % of portfolio

**Redemption terms:** None. No liquidity until fund liquidates positions.

**Secondary market:**
- Can sell LP stakes at discount (typically 10-30% below NAV)
- Secondaries market grew in 2024-2025 as LPs sought liquidity amid delayed exits
- Discount depends on fund quality, vintage, and market conditions

#### Fee Structure

**Management Fees:** 1.5-2.0% of committed capital (during investment period), then 1.5-2.0% of invested capital

**Performance Fees (Carry):** 20% of profits above 8% hurdle rate (typical)

**Total Cost:** 
- All-in fees typically 4-6% annually when including portfolio company fees
- **Critical:** Fees paid regardless of performance during first 7-10 years

**Fee Impact on Returns:**

Gross returns of 15% â†’ Net returns of 10-11% after fees (4-5% fee drag)

#### Operational Complexity

**Manager selection:**
- **Critical importance:** Top quartile returns ~20% annualized; bottom quartile ~0-5%
- Requires extensive due diligence (6-12 months per manager)
- Most top-tier funds closed to new investors
- Need existing relationships for access

**Ongoing monitoring:**
- Quarterly valuation reviews (but valuations are lagged/smoothed)
- Annual on-site visits
- Capital call management
- Distribution reinvestment decisions

**Governance burden:**
- Board reporting: quarterly performance, valuation methodology, exposure analysis
- Annual audits complex due to private valuations
- Regulatory reporting (Form 5500, state requirements)
- Legal/compliance: review of fund documents, side letters, conflicts

**Staffing requirements:**
- Dedicated PE team: minimum 2-3 FTEs for $200-500M program
- Support from legal, compliance, operations

### E. Capacity & Access

**Is PE capacity-constrained?**

Not overall, but access to top-tier managers is severely constrained.

- **Industry size:** $7 trillion globally; $3+ trillion in U.S.
- **Our scale ($500M-$2B):** Deployable, but challenging
  - Too large for small/mid-market funds (which often outperform)
  - Too small to be priority LP for mega-funds ($10B+)
  
**Barriers to entry:**
1. **Top-tier funds oversubscribed:** Blackstone, KKR, Apollo, Carlyle typically 2-3x oversubscribed
2. **Existing relationship advantage:** Funds favor re-ups from existing LPs
3. **Minimum commitments rising:** Mega-funds now require $50-100M minimum
4. **GP selection critical:** Median or below-median managers may not outperform public equity

**Quality dispersion:**

PE exhibits extreme manager dispersion:
- Top quartile: 18-22% net IRR
- Median: 12-14% net IRR
- Bottom quartile: 3-6% net IRR

**For pension funds:** Access to top-quartile managers is the entire value proposition. Without it, PE may underperform public equity after fees.

**Scale premium?**

Mixed. Large allocators get:
- Better access to top-tier funds
- Co-investment opportunities (lower fee exposure)
- Negotiated fee discounts (1.5% vs. 2.0% management fee)

But also face:
- Pressure to deploy large capital amounts quickly
- May sacrifice returns for check-size capacity

### F. Current Market Environment (2025)

**Valuation Assessment:** Expensive relative to historical norms

**Entry multiples:**
- Current buyout multiples: 11-13x EBITDA (vs. 8-10x historical average)
- Public market multiples: S&P 500 trading at ~20x P/E
- Implied limited room for multiple expansion on exit

**Exit environment:**

Severely challenged. Exit value in 2025 was $243.9 billion, below 2024's $120.4 billion and far below the 2021 peak of $527.8 billion. The number of exits (321 through December 2025) remains depressed. PE firms are sitting on 31,000 portfolio companies valued at $3.7 trillion.

**Implication:** Returns heavily dependent on operational improvements rather than multiple expansion.

**Fundraising trends:**

Fundraising hit a seven-year low in Q1 2025, $22 billion below prior year. Limited partners facing:
- Denominator effect (PE allocation crept up as public markets fell in 2022)
- Reduced distributions means less capital to re-commit
- Three years of below-expected payouts ($400B shortfall vs. expectations)

**Structural changes:**

1. **"Zombie funds" proliferating:** Funds 10+ years old still holding portfolio companies, collecting management fees with little prospect of good exits

2. **GP-led secondaries increasing:** GPs creating "continuation funds" to buy out LPs and extend hold periods

3. **NAV financing:** Managers offering early liquidity at discounts to paper NAV

4. **Fee pressure:** LPs demanding lower fees, more alignment (hurdle rates, clawbacks)

**Forward return expectations:**

Given current environment:
- High entry valuations
- Challenged exit markets
- Increased fee pressure
- Competition for deals

**Realistic expectation:** 10-12% net returns over next decade (vs. 13-15% historical)

## II. PRIVATE CREDIT (Direct Lending, Distressed, Mezzanine)

### A. Performance Characteristics

#### Historical Returns (2014-2025)

CalPERS reported private debt returned 12.8% for FY2024-2025. NYC pension funds' alternative credit (direct lending and asset-backed) delivered 9.5% average return across portfolios in FY2025, though most investments are floating-rate indexed to SOFR which adjusted lower as the Fed cut rates by 100 basis points.

**However, important context:** Callan Associates noted that "private credit, where many large institutional investors are increasing allocations, underperformed their public market equivalents (high yield and bank loans)" in the 2024-2025 period.

**Private Credit Asset Class Growth:**

Assets under management of private credit funds have grown at an average annual rate of 20% over the last five years, totaling $1.6 trillion, which accounts for 7% of the credit to non-financial corporations. Globally, private credit grew to $2.1 trillion in deployed and undeployed loan funds in 2023, with the vast majority of activity concentrated in the United States and Europe.

**Historical Performance by Strategy:**

| Strategy | Expected Return | Risk Profile | Current Yields |
|----------|----------------|--------------|----------------|
| **Senior Direct Lending** | 8-11% | Lower | SOFR + 400-550 bps |
| **Unitranche** | 10-13% | Medium | SOFR + 500-650 bps |
| **Mezzanine** | 12-15% | Higher | 12-15% cash + equity kickers |
| **Distressed Debt** | 10-20% | Highest | Highly variable |

**Crisis Performance:**
- **2008-2009 GFC:** Private credit funds experienced losses, but senior secured performed better than equity
- **2020 COVID:** Brief spread widening, but quick recovery; floating-rate benefit limited in zero-rate environment
- **2022-2024:** Strong performance as floating rates rose sharply; SOFR moved from near-zero to 5.3% before Fed cuts began

**Floating-Rate Advantage:**

Private credit's key feature is predominantly floating-rate structure:
- Loans typically SOFR + 400-600 bps spread
- **Benefit:** Protects against rising rates (income rises with policy rates)
- **Drawback:** Income falls when Fed cuts (as experienced in H1 2025)

### B. Diversification Benefits

**Correlation to Traditional Assets:**
- **Correlation to Public Equity:** 0.4-0.6 (moderate; driven by credit spreads and economic growth)
- **Correlation to High-Yield Bonds:** 0.7-0.8 (high; similar risk exposure)
- **Correlation to Investment-Grade Bonds:** 0.3-0.5 (moderate)
- **Correlation to 60/40 Portfolio:** 0.4-0.6

**Correlation Stability:**
- **Stable:** Correlations remain fairly consistent across market regimes
- **Rising rate environment:** Outperforms fixed-rate credit
- **Credit stress:** Underperforms public high-yield (illiquidity penalty)

**Does it protect during equity drawdowns?**

Partially. Private credit typically:
- Outperforms equity during downturns (senior position in capital structure)
- Underperforms investment-grade bonds (credit risk exposure)
- Correlates with high-yield bonds and leveraged loans

**Not a "safe haven" asset**, but provides some downside cushion relative to equity.

**Inflation protection?**

**Moderate to Strong** (in rising rate environments)

- Floating-rate structure provides automatic inflation adjustment
- Works well when inflation â†’ Fed tightening â†’ higher SOFR
- **Limitation:** In stagflation (high inflation + economic weakness), credit spreads widen, offsetting floating-rate benefit

### C. Return Drivers & Risk Factors

**Economic/Market Factors:**

1. **Policy Rates (75% of return):** Direct pass-through via floating rate
   - Current SOFR ~4.5% (down from 5.3% peak)
   - Spread: 400-600 bps
   - Total yield: 8.5-10.5%

2. **Credit Spreads (20% of return):** Compensation for default risk
   - Tight spreads (2024): investors demanding less compensation
   - Wide spreads (2008, 2020): investors demanding more compensation

3. **Default Losses (negative drag):** -1-3% annualized
   - Senior secured: <1% annual losses historically
   - Mezzanine/subordinated: 2-4% annual losses

4. **Alpha (manager skill):** 0-2%
   - Underwriting quality
   - Structuring and covenant protection
   - Workout/restructuring capabilities

**Risk Factor Exposures:**

| Factor | Exposure | Impact |
|--------|----------|--------|
| **Policy Rates** | Very High | ~1:1 pass-through to income |
| **Credit Spreads** | High | Widening spreads reduce valuations |
| **Economic Growth** | High | Defaults rise in recession |
| **Liquidity** | High | No secondary market; hold to maturity |
| **Inflation** | Positive | Benefits from higher rates (if Fed responds) |

**Is return from beta, alpha, illiquidity premium, or complexity?**

- **Beta (policy rates + credit spreads):** 85-90% of return
- **Illiquidity premium:** 50-150 bps vs. liquid high-yield
- **Complexity premium:** Minimal (strategies are straightforward lending)
- **Alpha:** 0-200 bps (from superior underwriting and structuring)

**Key Insight:** For about 70% of private credit deals, the borrowing company is sponsored by a private equity firm. This creates structural linkage between private credit and PE cycles.

### D. Institutional Implementation Considerations

#### Minimum Allocation Size

**For meaningful impact:**
- Minimum 3-5% of portfolio
- Below 2%: insufficient to justify operational complexity
- Optimal: 5-8% (balances return enhancement with liquidity management)

**Capital deployment:**
- Commitments to 5-10 funds across strategies
- $150-300M total commitment for our fund size
- Deploy over 2-3 years (slower ramp than PE)

#### Liquidity Profile

**Better than PE, but still illiquid:**

- **Fund life:** Typically 5-7 years (shorter than PE's 10-12 years)
- **Capital calls:** Drawn faster than PE (over 1-2 years vs. 3-5 years)
- **Distributions:** More regular (as loans mature and pay down)

**Secondary market:**
- Limited but growing
- Discounts typically smaller than PE (5-15% vs. 10-30%)

**Open-ended funds:**
- Some managers offer semi-liquid structures
- Quarterly redemptions with 60-90 day notice
- Gates may apply during market stress

**Key advantage vs. PE:** Cash yield provides regular distributions without requiring asset sales.

#### Fee Structure

**Typically LOWER than PE:**

**Management Fees:** 1.0-1.5% of committed capital or NAV

**Performance Fees:** 
- Direct lending funds: Often 10-15% carry (vs. PE's 20%)
- Distressed funds: Typically 20% carry
- Hurdle rates: 6-8%

**Total Cost:** 2-4% annually (vs. 4-6% for PE)

**Fee Efficiency:**
- Lower than PE due to simpler strategy
- Less operational complexity at portfolio company level
- Higher fee funds must justify through better deal flow or structuring

#### Operational Complexity

**Manager selection:**
- **Importance: High** (but less extreme dispersion than PE)
- Key criteria:
  - Origination capabilities (proprietary deal flow)
  - Underwriting discipline (through-cycle mindset)
  - Workout expertise (for inevitable defaults)
  - Alignment (GP co-investment)

**Ongoing monitoring:**
- Quarterly reporting (more timely than PE)
- Credit metrics: LTV ratios, interest coverage, covenant compliance
- Default management and workout situations
- Prepare for economic downturn: defaults rise 2-3x in recession

**Governance burden:** **Medium**
- Less intensive than PE (no operational involvement in portfolio companies)
- More predictable cash flows (floating rate + amortization)
- Valuation less subjective (loans have observable comparables)

**Staffing requirements:**
- Can be managed within existing credit/fixed income team
- 0.5-1.0 FTE dedicated to private credit for $150-300M program

### E. Capacity & Access

**Is private credit capacity-constrained?**

No. Market growing rapidly; ample deployment capacity.

Private credit funds have grown at 20% annually over the past five years. Supply of borrowers is large and growing as:
1. Banks retreated post-Dodd-Frank regulation
2. Middle-market companies (too small for bond markets) need capital
3. PE sponsors require debt for buyouts

**Our scale ($500M-$2B deployable):**
- Excellent fit for private credit
- Size range matches typical LP commitments ($25-75M per fund)
- Not too large (won't dominate single fund) or too small (can access quality managers)

**Barriers to entry:**

LOW. Private credit more accessible than PE:
- Newer asset class; less oligopolistic
- More managers; more capacity
- Track records shorter (harder to identify persistent winners)

**Quality concentration:**

Moderate performance dispersion:
- Top quartile: 12-14% net returns
- Median: 9-11% net returns
- Bottom quartile: 6-8% net returns

**Much narrower than PE**, making manager selection easier but still important.

**Scale advantages:**

Yes, but less pronounced than PE:
- Larger LPs get co-investment opportunities (lower fees)
- Better negotiating power on terms
- Access to proprietary deals from largest managers

### F. Current Market Environment (2025)

**Valuation Assessment:** Moderately Attractive

**Credit spread environment:**
- High-yield spreads: ~350-400 bps over Treasuries (tighter than historical average)
- Private credit spreads: 400-600 bps over SOFR
- **Implication:** Spread compensation moderate; relying on base rates for yield

**Policy rate environment:**
- SOFR currently ~4.5% (down from 5.3% in mid-2024)
- Fed cutting cycle: Expected to reach 3.75-4.25% by year-end 2025
- **Implication:** Total yields will compress from 10%+ to 8-9% if Fed continues cutting

**Supply/demand dynamics:**

Three-quarters of pension plans are invested in private market assets, and 86% of global respondents expect to be invested in private debt and private equity in three years' time.

**Strong demand drivers:**
1. Hunt for yield in post-ZIRP world
2. Floating-rate protection attractive after 2022 experience
3. Higher yields than investment-grade bonds with senior secured protection

**Structural changes:**

Private credit funds now account for 7% of credit to non-financial corporations, raising questions about systemic risks due to unregulated and opaque loan agreements.

**Regulatory concerns:**
- IMF and BIS flagging systemic risk concerns
- Potential for regulatory scrutiny
- Interconnections with banking system not fully understood

**Forward return expectations:**

**Base case:** 8-10% net returns over next 5 years
- Assumes SOFR averages 3.5-4.0%
- Spreads remain 400-500 bps

## VIII. RED FLAGS & COMMON PITFALLS

### Mistakes Pension Funds Make with Alternatives

1. **Over-allocating to illiquid assets**
   - Small plans' more liquid portfolios benefited as public equities generated double-digit returns in 2024, while private markets portfolios continued to play catch-up with their marks
   - **Denominator effect:** Alternatives allocation rises as % when public markets fall
   - **Liquidity crisis:** Unable to meet distribution needs or rebalance

2. **Chasing returns into expensive vintages**
   - PE in 2021-2022 at peak valuations
   - Infrastructure/digital assets in 2024-2025 (AI hype)
   - **Result:** Buying high, future returns compressed

3. **Underestimating governance burden**
   - Alternatives require 3-5x staff time vs. public markets
   - Board education and reporting demands
   - Manager monitoring and due diligence

4. **Fee leakage**
   - CalPERS paid at least $22M in fees on investment that lost 71%
   - Hidden fees at portfolio company level (PE)
   - Performance fees on underperformance (hurdle rates not met)

5. **Insufficient diversification**
   - Concentrating in 2-3 PE funds (vintage risk)
   - Geographic concentration

7. **Ignoring liquidity mismatches**

   - Forced selling in secondary market at discounts during crisis
